Tin Foil Boats

The Activity
Round 1

  • float on water
  • can hold coins
Discussion

What changes would make your design better?

This time around, you will have ten minutes to draw out your plan on a piece of paper. Then, you'll have another 10 minutes to assemble your tin foil boat. Before you test your boat in the water, describe your boat to the class and what changes you made (and why!).

LEDs and Circuits

Materials

This activity doesn't use the Arduino or computer at all. Instead we will be using our 9V battery to light up LEDs and learn the basics of circuits. You will need:

  • 9 volt battery
  • 9V battery connector (not in kit - will be handed out)
  • 4 LEDS of the same color
  • multiple 10kÎ© resistors
Breadboards

A breadboard lets us connect different electronics to build circuits without needing to wire every component together. A bread board is made up of strips. In the picture, each group of connected squares represents a strip. Sections A and D have two vertical strips each while sections B and C are made up of many shorter horizontal strips.

Components that are plugged into the same strip are essentially wired to each other. Generally, the vertical strips in section A and D are used for power and ground. The horizontal strips are used connecting the rest of the components that make up your circuit

Still confused? Need a refresher? Check out this Youtube Video on How to Use a Breadboard

If you are going to be using a chromebook for this class, you will be using Arduino Create to talk to your microprocessor. Arduino Create is an online IDE (interactive development environment).

General Arduino Create Steps

Once you have an account and are logged into Arduino Create, the website looks the same regardless of computer type. Everyone should follow the next 6 steps to run the Blink sketch on your Arduino -- this is the "hello world" equivalent on Arduino.

Step 1

After logging into Arduino Create, you should land on a page that looks like this.

Step 2

Connect the power cord to the Elegoo Uno R3 and then plug the other end into one of the USB ports

The Uno should light up one you plug in and there should be a small pop up saying Arduino Uno has been connected

Step 3

Inside your sketch, click on the black bar shown by the red arrow below.

If the first option in the dropdown menu says Arduino Uno at /dev/ttyACM#, click that so a blue check mark appears next to it. Skip to step 5!

If the first option is NOT Arduino Uno at /dev/ttyACM#, click Select Other Board & Port from the dropdown

Step 4

In the window that appears, search for and select Arduino Uno for your board. The ports menu is a little more complicated. Usually the software figures it out for you, but if it doesn't select the first option for now and then go back and change it if the next steps don't work (ask me for help if you're stuck). Click ok to accept the changes.

Step 5

Get ready to run your first sketch! Next to the black bar is a check mark. Click on the checkmark to verify your sketch. If everything is good to go, a green Success bar will pop up

Step 6

Final step - press the upload arrow next to the black bar. Again a green Success bar will pop up.

Look down at your Arduino. A yellow light should be flashing on and off every second!
